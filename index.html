624

private desks in the public space

Cultural belief: desks are for studio. The unstudio tests a different idea.

The solution

A collision space for those students.

The unstudio is a pilot for a collaborative solution to Gund's spatial constraints It's a place where students can spontaneously meet, share ideas, increase interdisciplinary connections, and design collaboratively — regardless of whether they're enrolled in a studio course.

It costs nothing. It uses furniture that already exists. It turns underutilized collaborative space into a resource for everyone.

Bring anything. Share everything.
Give up space. Don't hoard space.
Work with, not against.

Make it permanent

What you can do.

The unstudio closes in a matter of days. If it mattered to you — or if you believe it should matter to GSD — here's how to act.

01

Email your department head.

Let them know the unstudio served students across programs and ask them to advocate for making it permanent. Copy the message below.

Subject: Supporting the Unstudio at GSD I'm writing to advocate for making the unstudio — the pilot collaborative space in Gund Hall — a permanent feature of our school. Over a third of GSD students have no assigned home base in Gund each semester, while research shows that 89% of desks sit empty at any given time. The unstudio piloted a low-cost solution: repurposing an underutilized collaboration space to give all students access to a creative home base. In its first week alone, the space hosted 9 events and was visited over 80 times, with students from across all programs working side by side. The data is compelling. The community response has been enthusiastic. I'd ask you to advocate for continuing this experiment. It costs almost nothing and gives a lot.
Find faculty directory →
02

Email Dean Whiting.

Dean Whiting was the first to support this idea. Let her know the community values it and wants to see it continue.

Subject: The Unstudio — Please Make It Permanent Dear Dean Whiting, I'm writing in support of the unstudio initiative at GSD. What began as a student-led pilot has demonstrated real impact: a space where students from Architecture, Landscape, Planning, Design Studies, and other programs work side by side — spontaneously, productively, and joyfully. The data speaks clearly: Gund is at 2.5× capacity, over a third of students lack a home base, and yet an 89% desk vacancy rate suggests the problem is cultural and spatial, not scarcity. The unstudio offers a precedent for a better approach. I hope the school will commit to making this space — or something like it — a permanent part of GSD's spatial and pedagogical model. Thank you for your leadership in supporting this pilot.
